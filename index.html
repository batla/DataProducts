Synopsis

Storms and other severe weather events can cause both public health and economic problems for communities and municipalities. Many severe events can result in fatalities, injuries, and property damage, and preventing such outcomes to the extent possible is a key concern.

This project involves exploring the U.S. National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) storm database. This database tracks characteristics of major storms and weather events in the United States, including when and where they occur, as well as estimates of any fatalities, injuries, and property damage.

The NOAA Storm Data Explorer

Navigation

The Explore area allows you to Explore the NOAA Storm data by state or by year.

The user may choose to filter the data by a year range by adjusting the slider. In addition, the data may be filtered by event type.

Data by state is visualized as a Map of the United States. NOTE: In this first version, Hawaii & Alaska are excluded from the Map; however data is still available for those two states. In this view, the user may choose to filter by population or economic impact type.

Data by year is visualized by using line charts.

Data with current filters applied may be downloaded by the user for additional analysis of their choosing.

Data & Conclusions from EDA

Source Data

Data for the NOAA Storm Data Explorer is based off of the U.S. National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) storm database. Data was downloaded from this site. The data was then cleaned up and prepared for us by the NOAA Storm Data Explorer Application.

Fields not essential for the application were removed and data was aggregated by state and by year, with event types counted and impact summed.

The data used in this application, as well as the source code may be found on github.

Conclusions from earlier Exploratory Data Analysis

EDA was performed in an earlier project.

Analysis conducted from data collected by the NOAA between 1995-2011 shows that excessive heat and tornado events are most harmful to public health and flood, hurricane/typhoon, and storm surge events have the greatest negative economic consequences.
